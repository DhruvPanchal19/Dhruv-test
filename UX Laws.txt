1. Jakob's Law
Creator: Jakob Nielsen
Description: Users spend most of their time on other sites, so they prefer your site to work the same way as all the other sites they already know.
Examples:
E-commerce Websites: Sites like Amazon and eBay have similar layouts with search bars at the top, product categories on the left, and shopping carts in the top right corner.
Social Media Platforms: Facebook, Twitter, and LinkedIn have consistent navigation menus and profile layouts.
News Websites: CNN, BBC, and The New York Times use similar structures for headlines, articles, and navigation.

2. Fitts's Law
Creator: Paul Fitts
Description: The time to acquire a target is a function of the distance to and size of the target. Larger and closer targets are easier to hit.
Examples:
Mobile App Buttons: Large, easily clickable buttons on apps like Instagram's "Like" button.
Desktop Interfaces: The "Start" button in Windows is large and located at the corner for easy access.
Gaming Controllers: Buttons on game controllers are designed to be large and within easy reach.

3. Hick's Law
Creators: William Edmund Hick and Ray Hyman
Description: The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices.
Examples:
Google Search: A minimalistic homepage with a single search bar.
Apple's Product Pages: Simple and clean product selection pages with limited options.
Navigation Menus: Websites like Medium use concise and straightforward navigation menus.

4. Miller's Law
Creator: George A. Miller
Description: The average person can only keep 7 (plus or minus 2) items in their working memory.
Examples:
Phone Number Formatting: Breaking numbers into chunks (e.g., (123) 456-7890).
Menu Items: Limiting the number of items in a navigation menu to around 7.
Bullet Points: Using bullet points in presentations to keep information manageable.

5. Aesthetic-Usability Effect
Creators: Masaaki Kurosu and Kaori Kashimura
Description: Users often perceive aesthetically pleasing design as more usable.
Examples:
Apple Products: Known for their sleek, aesthetically pleasing designs.
Tesla's Interface: Clean and modern design of the car's dashboard interface.
Airbnb's Website: Visually appealing design that enhances usability.

6. Law of Proximity
Creator: Gestalt Psychology
Description: Objects that are near each other tend to be grouped together.
Examples:
Form Fields: Grouping related fields together in a form (e.g., name, email, phone number).
Navigation Menus: Grouping related menu items together.
Product Listings: Grouping similar products together on e-commerce sites.

7. Law of Similarity
Creator: Gestalt Psychology
Description: The human eye tends to perceive similar elements in a design as a complete picture, shape, or group, even if those elements are separated.
Examples:
Toolbar Icons: Similar icons in a toolbar (e.g., text formatting options in Microsoft Word).
Product Categories: Similar product images grouped together on an e-commerce site.
Social Media Feeds: Similar post formats on platforms like Facebook and Twitter.

8. Law of Common Region
Creator: Gestalt Psychology
Description: Elements tend to be perceived into groups if they are sharing an area with a clearly defined boundary.
Examples:
Sidebar Widgets: Bordered sections on a webpage, like the sidebar on a blog.
Card Layouts: Grouping related information in cards on dashboards.
Form Sections: Using borders to separate different sections of a form.

10. Serial Position Effect
Creator: Hermann Ebbinghaus
Description: Users have a propensity to best remember the first and last items in a series.
Examples:
Navigation Menus: Placing important menu items at the beginning and end.
Lists: Highlighting the first and last items in a list.
Presentations: Emphasizing key points at the start and end of a presentation.

11. Peak-End Rule
Creators: Daniel Kahneman and Barbara Fredrickson
Description: People judge an experience largely based on how they felt at its peak and at its end, rather than the total sum or average of every moment of the experience.
Examples:
Checkout Process: A memorable checkout process that ends with a thank-you message.
Customer Service: Ensuring the final interaction with customer service is positive.
Onboarding: Creating a strong first impression and a satisfying completion experience.

12. Von Restorff Effect
Creator: Hedwig von Restorff
Description: When multiple similar objects are present, the one that differs from the rest is most likely to be remembered.
Examples:
Call-to-Action Buttons: Highlighting a "Sign Up" button in a different color.
Special Offers: Using contrasting colors for promotional banners.
Error Messages: Displaying error messages in a distinct color to grab attention.

13. Doherty Threshold
Creators: Walter J. Doherty and Ahrvind J. Thadani
Description: Productivity soars when a computer and its users interact at a pace (<400ms) that ensures neither has to wait on the other.
Examples:
Loading Indicators: Progress bars or spinners that appear within 400ms.
Instant Feedback: Immediate visual feedback on button clicks.
Search Suggestions: Real-time search suggestions as users type.

14. Occam's Razor
Creator: William of Ockham
Description: Among competing hypotheses that predict equally well, the one with the fewest assumptions should be selected.
Examples:
Minimalist Design: Simple, straightforward interfaces like the Google homepage.
Clear Navigation: Avoiding unnecessary menu items and options.
Direct Messaging: Using clear and concise language in user interfaces.

15. Pareto Principle
Creator: Vilfredo Pareto
Description: For many events, roughly 80% of the effects come from 20% of the causes.
Examples:
Feature Prioritization: Focusing on the most used features in an app.
Bug Fixing: Addressing the most common issues that affect the majority of users.
Content Creation: Creating content that generates the most engagement.

16. Parkinson's Law
Creator: Cyril Northcote Parkinson
Description: Any task will inflate until all of the available time is spent.
Examples:
Project Deadlines: Setting clear deadlines to ensure tasks are completed efficiently.
Time Management: Allocating specific time slots for tasks.
Sprint Planning: Using agile methodologies to manage project timelines.

17. Tesler's Law
Creator: Larry Tesler
Description: Also known as the Law of Conservation of Complexity, it states that every application has an inherent amount of irreducible complexity.
Examples:
One-Click Purchasing: Simplifying complex processes like Amazon's one-click purchase.
Automated Workflows: Using automation to handle complex tasks.
User Onboarding: Streamlining the onboarding process to reduce complexity.

Web Browsers: Handling various HTML errors and still rendering the page correctly.
Form Validation: Accepting different date formats and standardizing them.
API Design: Designing APIs to be flexible in what they accept.

19. Goal-Gradient Effect
Creator: Clark Hull
Description: The tendency to approach a goal increases with proximity to the goal. Users are more motivated to complete tasks as they get closer to finishing them.
Examples:
Progress Bars: Showing progress in online surveys or forms, like the progress bar in Google Forms.
Loyalty Programs: Displaying points needed to reach the next reward tier, such as Starbucks Rewards.
Task Completion: Highlighting remaining steps in a multi-step process, like LinkedIn's profile completion meter.
_____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________
20. Zeigarnik Effect
Creator: Bluma Zeigarnik
Description: People remember uncompleted or interrupted tasks better than completed tasks. This can be used to encourage users to return to unfinished tasks.
Examples:
Task Management Apps: Showing incomplete tasks prominently, like Todoist.
E-commerce Carts: Reminding users of items left in their shopping cart, like Amazon's cart reminders.
Online Courses: Highlighting unfinished lessons to encourage users to complete them, like Coursera.
_____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________
21. Law of Uniform Connectedness
Creator: Gestalt Psychology
Description: Elements that are visually connected are perceived as more related than elements with no connection.
Examples:
Navigation Menus: Using connected background colors or borders to indicate related items, like the sidebar menu in Slack.
Form Sections: Grouping related form fields with borders or background colors, like Google Forms.
Dashboard Widgets: Using visual connections to group related data, like in Google Analytics.

22. Paradox of the Active User
Creators: John M. Carroll and Mary Beth Rosson
Description: Users prefer to start using a system immediately rather than spend time learning about it. This emphasizes the need for intuitive design.
Examples:
Software Onboarding: Providing intuitive interfaces that allow users to start using the software immediately, like Slack.
Mobile Apps: Designing apps that are easy to use without extensive tutorials, like Instagram.
Web Applications: Ensuring web apps are straightforward and require minimal learning, like Trello.

23. Cognitive Load Theory
Creator: John Sweller
Description: The amount of mental effort being used in the working memory. Designs should aim to reduce cognitive load to improve user experience.
Examples:
Dashboard Designs: Presenting information in a clear, organized manner to reduce cognitive load, like Google Analytics.
E-commerce Websites: Simplifying the checkout process to minimize cognitive load, like Amazon's one-click purchase.
Educational Platforms: Breaking down complex information into manageable chunks, like Khan Academy.
